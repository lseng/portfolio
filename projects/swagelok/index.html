Swagelok Web Catalog Design

Creating a website that holds hardware catalogs and documentations.

intro

Background

Swagelok is a multi-billion dollar hardware company that specializes in creating gas and fluid systems components, such as fittings, valves, tubing, hoses, and gauges.

01

The Problem

Swagelok has an out-dated, hardware-based system of exporting their catalogs describing how to use their products. With millions of customers, it becomes a huge cost to manufacter and make USB and CD based storage devices to send to their customers. Moreover, the catalog is not accessible.
The Task

I was tasked to create an electronic version of the catalog, using whatever medium that works best. As a pre-requisite, I had to do research on what are the best mediums for showing such information, and in which way.

02

Method Research

Website delivery methods are more accessible in the fact that users can access it from virtually any device given that that internet connection is available. As for application delivery methods, it requires more steps to access, but offers offline support, through having the ability to download and save offline content. Because of our primary goal to make this accessible to most people, we decided to opt for the website approach.
Website Research

Even within the realm of web development, certain website layouts present information better than others. According to Extended Themes, “a good layout keeps users on the site because it makes important information easily accessible and intuitive to find.” Likewise for a catalog design, it needs to have clear categories defined for users to easily find the information they are looking for.

 User Goals

When a user hops on the website, their goal is to see as much information as possible so they can search through what they’re looking for. They also need to have easy access to the thousands of catalog items, and be able to find what they’re looking for quickly.

03

Initial Design

The design utilizes a large, uncluttered canvas. Having white space grants easy visual direction towards what is important on the page. Psychologically, humans read in an F-Pattern, that is, they eyes scan any given webpage top-down where most attention is given to areas like an F shape on the page. Consequently, good design should utilize this to create good visual hierarchy.

White space on a catalog page and making use the F-shape for the menu.

 User Testing

I sent out a survey to random students at my high school and to see what they like and dislike about this design. Since it is only mid-fidelity, they had to picture what the final design would be like, and thus, could have skewed the results. However, since the functionality is the same between mid-fi and hi-fi, this round of user testing is most useful for feedback on how usable the website would be. The results for functionality were:
  • ~70% liked the hierarchical user flow
  • ~10% preferred to have no categories and just a search bar, like Google.
  • ~20% other mixed neutral responses.
